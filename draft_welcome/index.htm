Preserving and Exposing the Building Files

The Cambridge Historical Commission Building Files Collection is ten file cabinets full of information about every building in the city of Cambridge. The collection includes systematic survey information collected for each building in the city, Clippings, Postcards, Maps, Ephemera... The collection is actively managed with several new entries each week.

Each of the 40 file-cabinet drawers is filled with folders. Named and ordered according to Street Address or address-range. Some folders have additional information written on their name-tab to designate Named Place, such as specific Buildings, Streets and Squares, Canals, etc.

The C-Dash (Cambridge Digital Architectural Survey and History) project has developed a set of tools workflows for scanning each document in the building files. Through a semi-automated chain of procedures, the scanned files are transformed into a hierarchy of Documents, categorized by Document Type. Each document is related to a Place of a given Place Type. The product of this effort is a digital repository, catalog and map-based discovery system.

The easiest way to explain or understand CDASH would be to just start panning and zooming around on the map. Gray markers represent an Address. Colored markers repreent named locations. Hover over a marker to see the name of the place. Clicking a colored one. Notice the hierarchy of Places and related Documents. Each document references the name of its parent folder, which you can click to understand meaningful ways that documents are organized in the physical collection. Once you get the hang of it, you will see that a large proportion of the information that was put into the Building Files, has been successfully preserved in the digital collection which can now be explored by anyone in their home or while exploring the city itself.

The CDASH web site is based on the popular open-source digital archiving platform Omeka-S. The cusomized Omeka-S Theme and Modules behind CDASH are documented and made available for re-use at the CDASH c-dash.github.io.

Source: The Building Files

The Cambridge Digital Architectural Survey and History (CDASH) is a project to transform 10 file cabinets full of research material related to the architectural history of Cambridge (The Building Files) into a digital archive system that can serve as a vehicle for exploring the collection on the web and for further development of the information by historical commission staff.

The physical collection of Building Files contains approximately 16,000 folders, 70,000 documents comprised of 100,000 pages (based on an extrapolation from the first 29 of approximately 40 file boxes).

The building files is a hierarchy of location-specific folders containing various types of documents. The vast majority of folders are named for ranges of street-addresses. A subset of folders pertain to named places. e.g. Hale House. A smaller subset of folders refer to specific topics, e.g. City Hall Brick and Mortar Studies. Unmarked documents within a folder are presumed to relate to the address or named place marked on the folder. Some documents are marked with more specific addresses.

Destination: Omeka-S Digital Archive

The focus of the CDASH project has been to develop efficient, semi-automated process of digitzation, rough-sorting and geo-coding that will result in a digital archive designed using the popular open-source development and publishing tool Omeka-S.

The fundamental building block of an Omeka-S archive is an Item. An Item is like a catalog record, which can relate to a real-world object or a document. To address the management needs for a building files archive, the CDASH application defines two top-level categories of items: Place Items and Document Items.

Place Items

Place Items represent addresses or named places. Each Place item has coordinates that cause the place to be discoverable on the CDASH map. There are several sub-categories of named places (described below). Place items have many attributes that will allow an expert to add knowledge about a place, such as its type, and its architect, historical designation and temporal extent.

Each place item has a list of links to the Document Items that refer to that place.

Place Types

  • Addresses: Most of the places included in CDASH are of type: Address. Documents associated with addresses may refer to Buildings or other types of place that may currently or previouly occupied the location in question. The ambiguity of Address places may be cleared up by creating new named places in Omeka and re-assigning documents.
  • Named Places: There are several potential categories of names places. During the semi-automated import phase, these place names may be inferred from the names of folders. In later stages of the management of CDASH, place information may be further enhanced.
    • Buildings: e.g. City Hall, Joseph H Lord House, and many more.
    • Roads: Some folders are dedicated to photographs of streetscapes, for example, Mass Ave Quincy Square to Central Square
    • Parks
    • Monuments
    • Bridges
    • Canals

Document Items

Document Items represent documents of various types, described below. Each document is related to a place through a link. At the outset of scanning, CHC staff helped to establish a category scheme for the most common types of documents in the building files. Our rough sorting and digestion workflows have assigned each scanned page with a document item of one of the following types.

Document Types

The following document types were identified by the commission staff as the most common types of documents occuring in the building files. Each of the scans was grouped into items categorized as one of these types.

  • Architectural Inventory Form
  • Historical Building Survey
  • Research Form
  • Research Notes
  • Published Material
  • Exterior View
  • Interior View
  • Detail View
  • Plan View
  • Photo Contact Sheet
  • Correspondence
  • Demolition Memo
  • Supplemental Material
  • Ephemera

The hierarchy of Place and Document items allows information about named places to be developed and inherited by the documents related to that place.

Exploration and Discovery in Omeka-S

The primary means for discovering documents is exploring the map. Hovering over markers reveals the address ot the name of the place. Clicking brings up the page for the Place, with its list of related documents.

Places can be discovered through the Search Box at the top of the page.

Automated Population of Metadata and Potential for Enrichment

The semi-automated procedures for digitizing, and digesting the building files has resulted in some boiler-plate titles, subject keywords, and folder references being recorded for document items based on a semi-automated rough sort process.

A recent innovation for CDASH has been the assignment of Named Place and Topic information to Place and Document items associated with folders named for specific buildings, streets or other named places. Occasionally where a topic is specified in a folder name, this topic finds its way into the title or subject list for the associated documents.

Hidden Potential Attributes

There are lots of potential metadata attributes that can be specified for places and documents. These potential attributes are accessible through the edit interface for items but they invisible to users if they are not filled in.
